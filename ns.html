What is DES?

DES (Data Encryption Standard) is one of the earliest widely-used encryption algorithms. It is a symmetric-key block cipher, which means the same key is used to encrypt and decrypt data. DES was designed to secure sensitive data in computer systems and communications.

Developed by: IBM in the early 1970s
Standardized by: NIST in 1977
Purpose: To provide a standardized method of encrypting electronic data, especially in government and banking.

Key Features

  • Symmetric-key encryption
  • Block size 64 bits
  • Uses 56-bit key
  • 16 rounds of Feistel structure

How DES Works

DES operates on 64-bit blocks of plaintext using a 56-bit key. The process includes an Initial Permutation (IP), 16 rounds of Feistel function involving expansion, substitution (via S-boxes), permutation, and XOR operations, followed by a Final Permutation (FP) to produce the ciphertext. Decryption uses the same steps with subkeys applied in reverse order.
